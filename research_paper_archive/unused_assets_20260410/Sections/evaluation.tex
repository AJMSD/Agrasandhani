\section{Experimental Setup and Metrics}

\subsection{Workloads and Trial Matrix}

The primary evidence source is the replicated Intel sweep root \nolinkurl{final-intel-primary-replicated-20260408-135251}. It uses a bounded slice of the Intel Berkeley Research Lab deployment data \cite{intelLabData}, normalized into Agrasandhani's replay schema. The slice contains 4{,}320 replay rows drawn from 54 sensors. The primary matrix compares V0, V2, and V4 under MQTT QoS~0 and QoS~1 across five scenarios: \nolinkurl{clean}, \nolinkurl{bandwidth_200kbps}, \nolinkurl{loss_2pct}, \nolinkurl{delay_50ms_jitter20ms}, and \nolinkurl{outage_5s}. Each Intel condition is replicated three times with shared impairment seeds (\nolinkurl{53701}, \nolinkurl{53702}, and \nolinkurl{53703}) so the variants experience the same randomized impairments.

The portability check is the replicated AoT sweep root \nolinkurl{final-aot-validation-replicated-20260408-135251}, derived from a bounded Array of Things slice \cite{aotCyberGIS}. That validation slice contains 520 normalized rows drawn from 36 sensors. The AoT matrix is intentionally smaller: it compares V0 and V4 under QoS~0 for the clean and outage scenarios with two trials per condition. The AoT results are therefore treated as a directional consistency check rather than as a second primary claim source.

The replay harness accelerates both workloads by 5$\times$. Intel runs use a 30\,s wall-clock replay window, a 200-sensor target, and burst mode; AoT uses a 20\,s wall-clock replay window and a 50-sensor target. Unless otherwise noted, in-body table values are condition means across replicated trials. Supporting ablations use the packaged replicated sweep roots for the V2 batch-window sweep, the V1 versus V2 isolation sweep, the V2 versus V3 adaptive comparison, and the V3 parameter sweep.
In the QoS~0 headline matrix, the clean, bandwidth-capped, and outage frame-count patterns repeated exactly across trials, while the lossy case varied only at the one-frame level and preserved the same large gap between V0 and the smart variants.

The supporting sweeps are narrower by design. The V2 batch-window study varies only the fixed batch window while keeping the clean Intel workload and QoS~0 constant. The V1 versus V2 isolation sweep compares batching alone against batching plus compaction under matched batch windows for the clean, bandwidth-capped, and outage scenarios. The adaptive studies reuse the same Intel workload to compare V2 and V3 directly and then expand to a parameter grid over the send-slow threshold, the step-up increment, and the maximum adaptive window. These sweeps are used to interpret mechanism-level behavior, not to replace the primary Intel matrix.

\subsection{Impairment Model and Metrics}

Impairments are applied on the gateway-to-dashboard hop through the deterministic application-layer proxy. The clean scenario applies no impairment. The bandwidth-capped scenario limits the hop to 200\,kbps. The loss scenario injects 2\% packet loss. The delay/jitter scenario applies 50\,ms delay with 20\,ms jitter. The outage profile is phase-based, with 10\,s of steady delivery, a 5\,s complete outage, and a 45\,s recovery interval. The repository also includes optional host-level \texttt{tc netem} support, but the results reported here are grounded in the proxy-side impairment path and its outputs.

Latency is measured as \texttt{ts\_displayed - ts\_sent} and summarized with mean, p50, p95, and p99. Downstream traffic is measured at the impairment proxy as emitted frames and payload bytes. Freshness is interpreted as dashboard age of information. The canonical stability metric is the proxy inter-frame gap, together with proxy frame-rate variability; dashboard row cadence is not treated as a frame clock because the browser records only rendered events. For outage analysis, age over time is more informative than stale fraction alone because idle stale transitions are not sampled when the dashboard does not render new rows.

\input{tables/intel_main_summary_table.tex}

The strongest measured effect is downstream cadence reduction under last-hop impairment. Table~\ref{tab:main-summary} shows that across the four headline Intel QoS~0 scenarios, V0 emits between 116 and 132 downstream frames, whereas V2 and V4 emit between 4 and 6. Figure~\ref{fig:main-outage-frame-rate} illustrates the result most clearly in the outage case. During the five-second outage, V0 emits 116 downstream frames, V2 emits 4, and V4 emits 6, corresponding to an approximately 95--97\% reduction in downstream frame cadence. The gateway therefore reshapes a bursty dashboard stream into a paced sequence of aggregate updates.

  \centering

  \Description{A cumulative distribution of end-to-end display latency for the Intel QoS0 clean condition. The V0 curve rises quickly at low latency, while the V2 and V4 curves are shifted to the right, indicating substantially higher display delay.}
  \caption{Clean-path latency CDF for Intel QoS~0. Smarter variants paced the stream but increased display latency relative to V0.}
  \label{fig:latency-cdf}

The same mechanism imposes a substantial latency cost. Figure~\ref{fig:latency-cdf} and Table~\ref{tab:main-summary} show the clean-path trade-off: V0 reaches 31.9\,ms p95 latency, V2 reaches 276.2\,ms, and V4 reaches 607.7\,ms. That cost persists under impairment, so the positive result is paced rendering rather than faster delivery.

The bandwidth-capped case makes the mechanism more specific. Relative to clean, the 200\,kbps cap raises V0 p95 latency by 89.6\,ms, but leaves V2 and V4 within 35\,ms of their clean-path p95 values because those variants already throttle the downstream cadence before the impaired hop. The cap therefore hurts the raw stream more than the paced stream, even though the paced variants still send at least as many downstream payload bytes as V0.
The delay/jitter condition is interpreted in text and Figure~\ref{fig:inter-frame-gap-cdf}, rather than added to the condensed primary Intel table, because its main value is the shape of proxy timing variation rather than another row of the same frame-count contrast.

  \Description{A cumulative distribution of proxy inter-frame gaps for the Intel QoS0 delay and jitter scenario. The V0 baseline emits many closely spaced frames, while V2 and V4 shift the distribution toward far larger gaps because the gateway sends aggregated updates.}
  \caption{Proxy inter-frame-gap CDF for Intel QoS~0 under delay and jitter. The smart variants pace the dashboard stream by emitting far fewer downstream frames.}

The proxy-side stability metrics point in the same direction. In the Intel QoS~0 delay/jitter scenario, V0 has a proxy inter-frame-gap p95 of 79.8\,ms and a proxy frame-rate standard deviation of 3.81\,s$^{-1}$, while V2 and V4 emit at approximately one aggregate frame per effective batch window. The smart path therefore reduces visible burstiness by pacing the dashboard stream, not by making the impaired hop itself less variable.

Delay and jitter expose a complementary effect. In \nolinkurl{delay_50ms_jitter20ms}, V0 latency p95 rises to 1838.4\,ms, whereas V2 and V4 remain at 324.7\,ms and 645.4\,ms while emitting only five and six downstream frames. The gateway does not remove timing variation from the path itself; instead, it converts a bursty raw stream into a low-rate downstream sequence whose visible latency grows far less sharply under per-packet timing disturbance.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/intel_outage_qos0_v0_vs_v4_age_over_time.png}
  \Description{An age-of-information trace for the Intel QoS0 outage scenario comparing V0 and V4. V4 continues to display retained state during the outage, but its visible age remains much higher than the baseline both before and after recovery.}
  \caption{Outage age trace for Intel QoS~0. V4 keeps visible state through the outage, but the retained state is older than V0's visible state before recovery.}
  \label{fig:age-trace}
\end{figure}

V4 improves continuity rather than freshness. During the QoS~0 outage, V0 renders no updates while V4 renders 20 retained updates, allowing the dashboard to remain populated during the disruption. The visible state is older, however: pre-outage age p95 rises from 45.0\,ms in V0 to 489.0\,ms in V4, and recovery age p95 rises from 125.15\,ms in V0 to 278.3\,ms in V4. Figure~\ref{fig:age-trace} captures this distinction directly. V2 delivers most of the cadence reduction without retained-state semantics; V4 is useful when a stable last-known-good view is worth the additional age.

Comparing V2 and V4 clarifies where the extra cost originates. V4 emits only one or two more frames than V2 in the headline scenarios, so its additional benefit is not further cadence reduction. Instead, V4 layers retained-state continuity on top of the pacing already created by batching. That continuity costs another 260--330\,ms of p95 latency, depending on the scenario, and raises the age of the visible state both before recovery and after the hop becomes healthy again.

\subsection{Supporting Results and Negative Findings}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/intel_v2_batch_window_tradeoff.png}
  \Description{A trade-off plot for the Intel V2 batch-window sweep. Larger batch windows reduce downstream cadence but move the latency curve upward, indicating a direct latency-versus-stability trade-off.}
  \caption{Latency-versus-cadence trade-off across fixed V2 batch windows. The 250\,ms operating point sits near the knee between modest pacing and rapidly increasing latency.}
  \label{fig:batch-window-tradeoff}
\end{figure}

The batch-window sweep clarifies where the fixed-window operating point comes from. In the clean Intel V2 sweep, raising the batch window from 50\,ms to 1000\,ms increases latency p95 from 74.5\,ms to 1022.6\,ms while reducing maximum downstream frame rate from 3\,s$^{-1}$ to 1\,s$^{-1}$. The extreme 1000\,ms setting also introduces a non-zero stale fraction. This sweep shows that larger windows continue to pace the stream, but the latency penalty grows rapidly once the batch window moves beyond the 250\,ms operating point used in the main matrix.

The byte audit does not support a blanket payload reduction claim. In the four headline Intel QoS~0 scenarios, V2 increases downstream payload bytes by 3.4--8.1\% relative to V0, and V4 increases them by 43.7--66.3\%. The gateway therefore sends fewer but larger aggregate envelopes. The evidence supports a reduction in downstream frame cadence and burstiness, not a reduction in downstream payload-byte volume.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/intel_v1_vs_v2_isolation.png}
  \Description{A multi-scenario ablation figure comparing V1 and V2 across fixed batch windows. The two variants track closely across bytes, frame counts, and latency, indicating that batching explains most of the observed behavior.}
  \caption{V1 versus V2 isolation sweep. Under matched batch windows, batching explains most of the observed effect, and the incremental benefit of compaction remains small and mixed.}
  \label{fig:v1-v2-isolation}
\end{figure*}

The isolation and adaptive sweeps further narrow the story. In the V1 versus V2 isolation sweep, matched batch windows often yield identical downstream frames and bytes, and duplicate-sensitive counters remain at zero across the runs. Where differences do appear, they are small and mixed: under clean conditions V2 changes downstream bytes relative to V1 by -13.0\% to 0.0\%, while under outage it ranges from 0.0\% to +18.5\%. The measured effect beyond batching alone is therefore limited.

Likewise, the V2 versus V3 adaptive comparison shows no material advantage for the tested controller: stale fraction, frames, and bytes remain unchanged, the effective batch window stays fixed at 250\,ms, and the broader parameter sweep yields no scenario in which the controller leaves that operating point and improves stability without violating the latency or byte guardrails.
